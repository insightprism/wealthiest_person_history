## I. Executive Summary

Emperor Shenzong of Song presided over what many historians consider one of the most prosperous economies in world history during the late 11th century. His reign is marked by the ambitious Wang Anshi reforms (New Policies), which transformed Song fiscal policy through state monopolies, commercial taxation, and monetary expansion. The Song economy during this period is estimated to have had a GDP roughly three times that of 12th century Europe, with the first government-issued paper money, advanced trade networks, and unprecedented iron production. Coin minting peaked at 5.06 million guan in 1080 under his reign.

### 2. Base Output (B): **1.2 oz/person/year**

**Derivation Logic:**
The Song economy was exceptionally productive for its time:
- GDP per capita estimated at $450-600 (1990 international dollars), higher than Western Europe
- Per capita iron output rose sixfold between 806 and 1078
- Kaifeng Fu (capital region) had GDP per head of ~$1,850, on par with highest medieval European levels
- Economic historian Cheng Minsheng estimated average lower-class labor income at 100 wen/day, ~5x subsistence level
- China's per capita income was higher at end of Song than under Mao in 1950s

Given the exceptional economic development, high urbanization, and advanced manufacturing (especially iron), a base output of 1.2 oz is justified—above the default 1.0 but not at the highest levels reserved for industrial economies.

### 4. Extraction Margin (M): **0.15**

**Derivation Logic:**
The Wang Anshi reforms significantly increased state extraction:
- Defense consumed 83% of cash income, requiring high extraction
- Industrial and commercial taxation exceeded agricultural taxation
- State monopolies on salt, tea, wine generated substantial revenue
- Annual fiscal intake peaked at 150 million guan during Tianxi era
- In 1120, Song collected 18 million ounces (510,000 kg) of silver in taxes
- Merchants paid ~3.3% (1/30th) of profit as tax
- New Policies gave Shenzong "a large budget surplus"

Revenue composition around 1160:
- Salt tax: 50%
- Wine tax: 36%
- Tea tax: 7%
- Customs: 7%
- Total monopoly revenue: ~40 million strings annually

Given the combination of monopolies, commercial taxes, and agricultural levies, 15% extraction is appropriate—heavy by pre-modern standards but reflecting the sophisticated Song fiscal apparatus.

### 5. Capitalization Multiple (K): **15**

**Derivation Logic:**
Adjusting below the default 20 because:
- Constant military pressure from Liao and Western Xia
- Wang Anshi reforms generated political instability
- Factional conflict between reformers and conservatives
- Reforms were abolished shortly after Shenzong's death (1085)
- Eventually Northern Song fell to Jurchen Jin (1127)
- Empire lasted only ~40 years after Shenzong's death

The political instability and external threats warrant a lower multiple of 15x.

**Confidence Score:** **High** (methodological adjustment based on documented instability)

### 6. Liquid Treasury (L): **5,000,000 oz**

**Derivation Logic:**
Treasury reserves are difficult to estimate directly, but evidence suggests:
- Wang Anshi reforms gave Shenzong "a large budget surplus"
- Reserve Treasury (deposit treasury) established by Emperor Taizu
- Annual silver tax collection in 1120: 510,000 kg (18 million oz) of silver
- Peak revenue: 150 million guan annually
- Budget showed deficits at times (25 million guan deficit during Song Zhenzong)

Converting to gold ounces:
- Using historical gold:silver ratio of ~8:1 for Song China
- If annual silver revenue was ~500,000 kg, treasury reserves might accumulate to several million oz gold equivalent
- Estimate: 5 million oz gold equivalent treasury reserve

This is a rough estimate based on annual revenue flows and historical accumulation patterns.

## IV. Expected Calculation Preview

Using the SAVE formulas:

**Income Valuation:**
- AGR = N × B × V = 100,000,000 × 1.2 × 6.0 = 720,000,000 oz

**Total Real Wealth:** 1,620,000,000 + 11,000,000 = **1,631,000,000 oz** (~1.63 billion oz)
