## I. Executive Summary

Mir Osman Ali Khan, the seventh and last Nizam of Hyderabad, was reputed to be the richest man in the world in the early 1940s, with wealth estimated at US$2 billion (~2% of US GDP at the time). His private treasury reportedly contained £100 million in gold and silver bullion plus £400 million in jewels. The source of this legendary wealth was the Golconda diamond mines—the world's sole supplier of diamonds at that time. He ruled Hyderabad State, the largest and wealthiest princely state in British India, covering 82,698 square miles with a population of 16.34 million.

Using 150 million oz as combined value of:
- Gold bullion holdings
- Silver reserves
- Diamond and jewel collection
- Cash and securities

This high figure reflects:
- Golconda as world's sole diamond source

## VII. Caveats and Alternative Estimates

**Unique Status:**
The Nizam was simultaneously:
- A sovereign ruler (own army, currency, foreign relations)
- A British protectorate (paramountcy)
- A private wealth holder (personal treasury)

This makes valuation complex.

**Wealth Estimate Variations:**
- 1940s estimate: $2 billion (Time magazine)
- 2024 adjusted: $38.5 billion
- At $2,900/oz gold: ~13 million oz (if purely gold)
- Our estimate: 200+ million oz (including all assets)

**Population Alternatives:**
- 1941 census is reliable: 16.34 million

**Treasury Alternatives:**
- Lower (50,000,000 oz): Conservative gold-only
- Higher (250,000,000 oz): Maximum valuations

**The "Richest Man" Claim:**
The Nizam's status as "richest man in the world" was widely reported and he appeared on Time's cover. However:
- Wealth mixed personal and state assets
- Diamond valuations fluctuate
- Colonial context limited full sovereignty

**Legacy:**
- Donated 5,000 kg gold to India during 1962 Sino-Indian War
- Jewels of the Nizams now in National Museum, Delhi
- Jacob Diamond remains one of world's largest (185 carats)
- State was forcibly annexed in Operation Polo (1948)
