## I. Executive Summary

Pope Alexander VI, born Rodrigo de Borja (Italianized as Borgia), was one of the most controversial and wealthy popes in history. A Spaniard from the Kingdom of Valencia, he accumulated vast wealth during 35 years as Vice-Chancellor of the Church before buying the papacy in 1492 with "four mule-loads of silver." Giovanni de Medici reportedly said, "Now we are in the power of a wolf, the most rapacious perhaps that this world has ever seen." His reign was marked by unprecedented nepotism, simony (selling church offices), and the military campaigns of his son Cesare Borgia to carve out a Borgia kingdom in central Italy. Cesare conquered the Romagna (1499-1503), inspiring Machiavelli's *The Prince*. Alexander's wealth came from multiple streams: worldwide church revenues (annates, Peter's Pence, fees for documents), Papal States taxation, sale of cardinalates, and Borgia family benefices across Spain and Italy.

### 4. Extraction Margin (M): **0.30**

**Derivation Logic:**
Pope Alexander VI had extraordinary extraction capabilities:

**Direct Revenue Sources (Papal States):**
- Land taxes on Papal States territories
- Tribute from vassal states and vicars
- Customs and tolls
- Salt monopoly revenues

**Ecclesiastical Revenue (Worldwide Church):**
- Annates: "tax usually amounting to about one-half the gross annual income" of new benefice holders
- "In 1521 these fees [annates] amounted to 13 percent of papal income"
- Peter's Pence from Northern Europe
- Fees for documents: "one-third to one-half of the pope's disposable income"
- Sale of venal offices: "10 to 15 percent of papal revenues"
- Indulgences and dispensations

**Alexander VI's Particular Extraction:**
- Notorious for simony - selling cardinalates
- Created 47 new cardinals (each paying substantial fees)
- Confiscated property of deceased/disgraced prelates
- Sold offices to highest bidders
- Giuliano della Rovere spent 200,000 gold ducats trying to outbid him for papacy

Using 0.30 because:
- Normal papal extraction from Papal States: ~15-20%
- Church revenues from worldwide sources added significant income
- Alexander's aggressive simony and office-selling increased extraction
- "Bribery and simony to an art form"
- One of history's most extractive popes

## VII. Caveats and Alternative Estimates

**Unique Position:**
Pope Alexander VI presents a unique case for wealth valuation because:

1. **Dual Nature of Papal Power:**
   - Temporal ruler of Papal States (direct control)
   - Spiritual head of Catholic Church (worldwide revenues)
   - The calculation focuses primarily on temporal wealth, but church revenues significantly enhanced extraction

2. **The Borgia Project:**
   Alexander's goal was to create a hereditary Borgia principality in Italy. Cesare conquered the Romagna with the plan of becoming an independent ruler. This failed instantly upon Alexander's death, illustrating why the capitalization multiple is low.

3. **The "Worst Pope" Debate:**
   Alexander VI is frequently cited as the most corrupt pope in history. His contemporaries called him:
   - "The most rapacious wolf" (Giovanni de Medici)
   - Girolamo Savonarola tried to have his election voided for simony

**Population Alternatives:**
- Lower estimate: 1,500,000 (Papal States core only)
- Higher estimate: 4,000,000 (including all conquered Romagna)
- Used: 2,500,000 (middle estimate at peak)

**Extraction Alternatives:**
- Lower (0.20): Standard papal extraction
- Higher (0.40): If including full exploitation of simony

**Treasury Alternatives:**
- Lower (2,000,000 oz): Conservative estimate
- Higher (10,000,000 oz): If including all benefice wealth

**Comparison to Other Religious Leaders:**
- Pope Alexander VI (1500): ~83 million oz
- King Solomon (950 BC): ~1.14 billion oz
- The Papal States were small compared to great empires

**Why Relatively "Low" Wealth:**
Despite his legendary corruption, Alexander VI's wealth was limited by:
- Small territorial base (Papal States ~2.5 million people)
- Short-lived Borgia conquests
- Non-hereditary succession (capitalized value reduced)
- Much wealth spent on wars, nepotism, luxury

**Machiavelli's Assessment:**
Niccolò Machiavelli spent months at Cesare Borgia's court (1502-1503) and was deeply impressed by his ruthlessness and political skill. *The Prince* was partly inspired by Cesare's methods. However, Machiavelli also noted how quickly Borgia power collapsed when Fortune turned against them—a lesson in the fragility of power built on a single life.

**Legacy:**
The Borgia name became synonymous with corruption, nepotism, and ruthless ambition. Alexander's great-great-grandson, St. Francis Borgia (1510-1572), became a Jesuit saint—perhaps in atonement for the family's sins. The Borgia Apartments in the Vatican, decorated by Pinturicchio with Alexander's portrait, remain a testament to his patronage of the arts amid his political machinations.
